What are Self-Driving Systems?

Autonomous driving is a feature of offered by certain automotive companies like Tesla, Audi, Wayamo etc.
This feature allows the driver to use autonomous driving/assistance to help drive their vehicles. First instance of autonomous assistance was in 1948, where Ralph Teetor invented the first cruise-control system.



However, major progress began in the 1980's where, via the funding from both the US government and German government, the first semi-autonomous driving system was developed.
Through later developments in the early 2000's and 2015, the first autonomous driving system was developed.
From 2017 onwards, companies like Waymo developed privately researched autonomous driving systems. And then, further advancements made by Tesla in the 2020's, Full Self Driving (FDS) systems were now capable of reliably driving without human input.

SAE Levels of Driving Automation

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) defines autonomous driving levels through a standard called J3016, which classifies autonomous vehicle capabilities into five levels, ranging from Level 0 (no automation) to Level 4(FSD) (full automation). These levels help to clarify the degree of automation in a vehicle and the amount of human intervention required. Here’s a breakdown of how the SAE defines these levels:

Level 1

Driver Assistance

Adaptive Cruise Control (ACC). The system controls either acceleration/braking (but not both simultaneously). The driver must always keep their hands on the wheel and monitor the road.

Level 2

Partial Automation

Example: Tesla Autopilot (2.0), Lane Keeping Assist (LKA) systems. The vehicle can simultaneously manage steering and acceleration/braking. The driver must remain ready to take control at any moment.

Level 3

Conditional Automation

Example: Audi A8 with Traffic Jam Pilot, Tesla Autopilot (3.0), The system can take full control in specific conditions (e.g., in traffic jams). The driver can be distracted but must be ready to intervene if prompted by the system.

FSD

High Automation

Example: Robotaxi prototypes (Waymo, Yandex,advanced Tesla FSD Beta versions). The vehicle can fully drive itself in specific geofenced areas and conditions without driver intervention. The driver can completely relax, but the system is limited to predefined scenarios.

Determining the Levels:

The SAE levels are based on two main factors:

  • Driving Task Automation: How much control the vehicle has over driving functions (steering, acceleration, braking, etc.).
  • Driver's Role: The extent to which the driver must remain engaged, monitor the environment, and be ready to intervene.

As the level increases, the vehicle takes on more of the driving tasks, and the driver’s involvement decreases, with Level 4 representing complete autonomy where no human intervention is needed at all.



Pros and Cons

Pros

1. Enhanced Safety:

  • Reduced Human Error: Automated Systems reduce human error with driving such as fatigue
  • Advanced Senses: Sensors and advance algorithms allow pre-emptive detection of hazardous situations and course correct

2. Enhanced Convenience

  • Reduces stress while driving, especially in congested areas
  • Allows occupants to do other tasks while travelling

3. Accessibility

  • Allows people who cant drive, for example people with disabilities or elderly people, to drive safely

Cons

1. Technical difficulties

  • Current FSD systems struggle in complex or unpredictable driving conditions, such as heavy rain, snow, or poorly marked roads.
  • Limitations in AI’s ability to handle nuanced human decisions (e.g., ethical dilemmas or interpreting subtle gestures)

2. Operating Costs

  • Maintenance costs and operating costs are difficult to maintain.
  • Costs for AI development and Data centres for AI make it hard to implement FSD systems

3. Legal Issues and Public trust

  • Many core modules and systems of FSD systems are still debated in courts for their usability.
  • Public opinion is still against the idea of having autonomous-driving cars
