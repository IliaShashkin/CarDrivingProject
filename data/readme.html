It is, once more, time for a little group exercise!

1. Scenario

This time you will implement classical haggling experience at a market stand. You may imagine any antique, oriental, eastern, western, medieval or modern market you’d like to think of — the goal is always to come to an agreement about how much a product is worth.

Contrary to a store where prices are usually fixed, at a market it is customary to not accept the price the vendor asks for right away. Instead, the customer makes a counter-offer, countered again by the vendor, and so forth until an agreement is reached — or the sale fails.

Such a system is even occasionally implemented in your favorite thing: video games.

We now want to implement a similar application (with a little less advanced graphics 😉). To do this you will be assigned to one of four teams:

  1. One team will implement the customer

  2. Another team will implement the vendor

  3. A third team will take care of a user-interface showing the progress and outcome

  4. Finally, a tech-team will make sure that the 'messages' are passed between customer, vendor and display

2. Interaction

All teams will implement their part independently — with just a little oversight from the tech-team!

The goal of the assignment is to, in the end, combine all four parts into one application and watch as our virtual customer & vendor haggle with each other automatically. To allow independent implementation while still keeping the different systems compatible we need contracts.

So one of the most important parts of this exercise is to define contracts and then implement against those without making additional assumptions. A contract is, of course, an interface!

This does not only include the interaction, but also the available products and their properties, currency ranges (and denomination),…​

3. Steps

We’ll do this assignment in multiple steps:

  1. Group Assignment — get together at one end of the classroom once you’ve been assigned

  2. Initial group-internal discussion

    • Read through the description of your team’s task below

    • Make sure all team members understand the team’s task

    • Come up with a rough idea how you want to implement it

    • Create an initial proposal for contracts

  3. Initial group-conference

    • Each group presents its proposal for the interfaces (one or more)

    • Other groups can give feedback if they feel something is missing, superfluous, or they would prefer to design it differently

  4. Agreement on interfaces

    • All groups will agree upon:

      • The interfaces

      • The shared model (e.g. enums, (message) objects,…​)

    • The tech-team will then set up a GitHub repository which contains these types

      • Other teams have just read-only access

  5. Within your group, work on the implementation of your task

    • If it helps you, you can create sample/dummy implementations or mocks for the interfaces of other teams

    • Unit tests may be very useful!

  6. In the next lesson we will do another round of discussion

    • Did any issue with the previously agreed interfaces turn up?

    • Are changes required?

      • Remember: that will cause additional work for all teams, so better be right the first time 😉

  7. Complete implementation

    • Important each team should create a class library project which will result in a DLL — make sure to check with the tech-team to ensure all will work together in the end

  8. In the next lesson we will have the finale

    • The teach-team will receive the individual implementations and combine them into one project

    • We will start this project, hooked up to the beamer, and watch the haggling!

  9. We will probably see some balancing issues, maybe minor technical ones as well 🙄

    • A short emergency-fixing-session will be permitted

  10. Final Run — all should work well now! 😎🎉

4. Team Tasks

You have some leeway on how to implement your logic, but stick to the rough rules stated below to ensure the application it will work in the end.

It is sufficient if you read only your own section, you don’t have to worry about the others' tasks

4.1. Customer

  • Different customers have different likes and dislikes

    • For example some might love fruit which others hate, while yet another is on the hunt for a porcelain vase

    • This will be influenced by the range and properties of the products all teams agree upon

      • Make sure you get enough 'in' that you can work with it in a meaningful way

  • Implement at least two different customers 'types'

    • Bonus points if you manage to generate customers with random likes, dislikes and amount of money

  • A customer has only a certain amount of money

    • They can’t spend more than that

    • Maybe they don’t want to spend everything on only one product, because they want to buy something else as well

    • They might spend more on something they really, really want

    • Rare things might be worth more, than something abundant?

  • Your goal is to pay as low a price as possible

    • But you also don’t want the sale to fail (except if you hate the product 😉)

    • ⇒ Make sure not to be too aggressive, or the vendor may get angry and refuse to sell you anything

  • Achieving a very good price may make a customer happy — they could decide to buy something else from the same vendor

4.2. Vendor

  • Some vendors are more patient than others

    • Once patience runs out the vendor gets angry and aborts the sale

    • Too bold demands from the customer can make a vendor impatient faster

      • Be aware, that outrageous demands from the vendor may make the customer grumpy as well

  • A vendor is possibly nicer if the customer is a little child asking to buy some fruit, compared to the customer being an unfriendly adult haggling for a carpet

  • The vendor knows how much they paid for a certain item

    • So they also know what’s the min. price they have to get in order to not make a loss with the sale

    • However, a very happy customer may buy something else as well?

  • Stock amount of a certain product may affect the price — scarce items may yield a higher profit

  • Remember: if the sale fails the customer goes home without a new product, but the vendor won’t have made any money — and they need to make money to afford cost of living for them and their family

    • ⇒ A vendor is very motivated to sell something

    • Balance not having any income with make a smaller profit

  • Some products might be perishable, so a vendor will try to sell them, even at a lower price, instead of throwing them away

4.3. Display / UI

  • Your job is to make the interactions between customer(s) and vendor(s) visible to the user — in a nice way

  • Important decide with the other teams if user interaction should be possible or if the whole process will be handled automatically by the customer and vendor agents

    • If user input is possible it should be limited to e.g. a product selection and not interfere in the haggling process

  • We don’t know how to create graphical UIs yet, but in the previous assignment you learnt about the Spectre.Console package

    • Use this package to create a nice looking console application

    • Use features like progress, charts, colors, images, emojis,…​ to present information

  • To emphasize events (e.g. a happy 😁 or angry 😤 person) you’ll not only have to use some proper UI features, but you also need to know how to recognize such things

    • That will have to go into the interface & model discussion

  • You have to represent the whole process, so make sure you are aware of all the steps

    • Also make sure you’ll receive all the necessary information to properly present the events

  • Your team does not have to implement a lot of logic, but you have to come up with a good way of representation

    • You also have to understand what will go on between customer & vendor to be able to plan and implement the UI

  • The UI may have to wait for user input or something else to complete — make sure to communicate well with the tech team during team-conferences to ensure they are aware of your needs

    • You’ll have to deal with some advanced async & await stuff — I’ll help you with that

4.4. Tech Team

  • Your team has a bit of a special role: you aren’t tasked with implementing any specific part of the application, but instead you’ll work on the 'glue' needed to put it all together in the end

  • You are keeper of, and final authority on the interfaces and the model in general

    • No other team can modify the contracts,

    • You only modify them if the team-conference agrees upon it

      • If the vote is split, you make the ruling

  • Always keep in mind that in the end you’ll receive three DLLs which you’ll have to reference in the final application and 'link' them all together

    • Ensure that interfaces & model are capable of the things you need

    • Maybe consider a 'messaging' model in which the central part of the application (yours) acts like a 'broker'

  • In addition to the design questions there are also some technical challenges

    • Helping the other teams set up class library projects and merging it all together in the end

    • Dealing with delays from the UI

      • We might have to wait for user input

      • Even if not: if the vendor and customer make a deal within 5 milliseconds the users won’t see much of it ⇒ delays will be required to allow the audience to follow the chain of events

      • You’ll have to deal with some advanced async & await stuff — I’ll help you with that

5. Grading

In general, you will be graded based upon:

  • Teamwork & project management

    • How well are you working together, is work distributed fairly, are you meeting your milestones on time

  • Task fulfillment

    • How well have you fulfilled your team’s specific task?

  • Quality of the implementation

    • How good is your solution?

  • Participation in discussions

    • Did you contribute something useful to the coordination discussions?

If a team’s solution fails to integrate with the other (= contracts not properly implemented & consumed) that would be a failing grade for the team!
